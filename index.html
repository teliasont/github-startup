Project Identity Card

Title: Taste Without Flatness

Curiosity statement: Can AI learn to hold the tension in a person’s taste instead of resolving it into a clean profile?

Summary: An investigation into AI as a personal taste interpreter — how AI tools surface, flatten, or distort individual aesthetic identity. The project asks whether an AI-driven interface can preserve the specificity and contradiction that make someone’s taste genuinely theirs.

Audience: Designers, researchers, and AI practitioners working at the intersection of personal identity and interface design.

Possible final directions:

  • A speculative interface prototype that maps taste through juxtaposition rather than averaging
  • A comparative analysis of how current recommender systems model (and misrepresent) preference
  • A critical essay + visual system exploring AI and aesthetic identity

Inquiry Brief

Research questions:

  1. How can AI interpret and reflect personal taste without flattening what makes that taste specific and unique to one person?
  2. How can we design an AI-driven interface that maps an individual’s taste profile through contextual juxtaposition rather than algorithmic flattening?

Why it matters: Most recommendation engines optimize for engagement, not fidelity to a person’s actual taste. The result is a kind of aesthetic averaging — a flattened version of the user. This project asks what a more honest AI interface might look like: one that holds contradiction, specificity, and context rather than collapsing them into a score.

What I need to learn: How current AI systems model preference (embeddings, collaborative filtering, content-based approaches); what “contextual juxtaposition” means as a design method; case studies of taste-aware or anti-recommendation interfaces.

Possible outcomes: Interface prototype, critical essay + visual system, or speculative design artifact.

Source Starter List

  • Kyle Chayka, Filterworld: How Algorithms Flattened Culture (2024) — Book — Argues that algorithmic recommendation homogenizes taste rather than revealing it. Central to understanding the problem this project is responding to.
  • Recommendation systems research (paper TBD) — Academic — Explains how collaborative filtering and embedding models construct user preference; reveals the assumptions baked into “personalization.”
