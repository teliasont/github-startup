Taste Without Flatness

AI as a personal taste interpreter — Tayla Eliason

Project Identity Card

Working title: AI Personal Taste Interpreter

Curiosity statement: Where can AI be used to learn more about people’s personal tastes and implement that knowledge in a way useful to the individual without flattening or homogenizing their unique interests?

Summary: I am quite fond of odd stats about myself — like my music taste, or how many books I read in a month. It helps me realize and contextualize thoughts about myself so I can properly describe my interests or leanings. I’d be curious to see how AI could take information about one’s self and provide recommendations without homogenizing it. The goal is to keep the data specific to the person, since everyone is going to have very different and incredibly specific data. Personalization is a very interesting topic, for both its benefits and drawbacks.

Audience: People who love their personal stats — Spotify Wrapped, reading logs, For You feeds. Stats for nerds.

Possible final directions:

  • Bookshelf / CD organizer
  • Library card pitch tool
  • Music taste interpreter
  • Something tangible

Inquiry Brief

Research question: How can AI interpret and reflect personal taste without flattening what makes that taste specific/unique to one person?

Why it matters: Personalization is everywhere — For You pages, Spotify Wrapped, the ads on your devices. Many AI-powered systems push users toward the popular. This project asks whether AI can do the opposite: amplify what is particular and eclectic about a person rather than smooth it out.

What I need to learn:

  • How existing taste-interpretation tools work and where they fall short (Spotify, stats.fm, Obscurify, Goodreads, etc.)
  • What personalization looks like when it goes wrong — homogenization, filter bubbles, loss of specificity
  • Where AI genuinely adds something a filter or sort can’t — pattern recognition, language, inference
  • What people actually want to know about themselves that current tools don’t tell them

Possible final outcomes:

  1. Music taste interpreter — an AI-powered tool that analyzes listening history and surfaces personal insights in language, not just charts; focused on pattern, evolution, and context over time
  2. Bookshelf / CD organizer — a practical collection tool where AI reads your physical media, builds a catalog, and generates recommendations that stay true to the specificity of your taste
  3. Library card pitch tool — a personalized experience that takes your interests and maps them to what your local library actually offers, making a specific case for why you should have a card

Source Starter List

  • Kyle Chayka, “The Age of Algorithmic Anxiety” (The New Yorker) — Essay — Explores the psychological “flattening” effect of algorithms and the unease of being read by a machine that prioritizes the average over the unique.
  • Kyle Chayka, Filterworld: How Algorithms Flattened Culture (2024) — Book — Covers how algorithmic recommendation engines create a “global blandness” and what resisting Filterworld looks like. The definitive text for the “what I’m fighting against” section.
  • Maggie Appleton, “The Dark Forest and Generative AI” / “The Expanding Dark Forest and Generative AI” — Articles — The Cozy Web / Dark Forest framing for how people seek authentic connection online as AI floods public spaces with generated content.
  • Eugene Wei, “TikTok and the Sorting Hat” + related essays (2020–2021) — Blog posts — Argues TikTok’s interest-graph algorithm hyper-sorts users into niche micro-communities rather than homogenizing them; a critical counter-argument to pure flattening.
  • Sutton & Barto, Reinforcement Learning: An Introduction Ch. 2 — “Multi-armed Bandits” — Academic — The exploration vs. exploitation framework explaining how recommender systems balance safe popular content against risky new content.
  • Stephen Wolfram, “How to Think Computationally About AI, the Universe and Everything” (TED, 2023) — Video — Proposes shifting from how to do it to why to do it in computational thinking; useful context for how AI reasoning is structured.

Prompt Log

Entry 1

Model: Gemini

Prompt: Research question + context, asking Gemini to push back on the assumption that algorithms inherently flatten culture, identify 3–5 more specific questions, surface unconsidered angles, flag contested debates, and suggest sources — then continued as a multi-turn design dialogue on input mechanics, output format, and interaction design.

Output summary: Gemini reframed the core assumption (flattening is a business model choice, not a feature of AI); introduced pairwise comparison as the right input mechanic over star ratings; proposed the semantic constellation (AI-generated custom axes + spatial data visualization) as the output format; flagged exploration vs. exploitation and algorithm aversion as contested areas worth investigating.

What changed: Moved from “algorithms flatten taste” to “AI can be designed to amplify the specific.” Eliminated star ratings and broad categories as design directions. Committed to a visual output with user-specific axes rather than generic charts. Pairwise comparison — asking context-anchored questions like “You are snowed in with the power out: do you reach for Book A or Book B?” — emerged as the core interaction design mechanic.
